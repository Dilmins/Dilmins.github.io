Brain controlled assistive exoskeleton arm for hand impaired individuals

A brain–computer interface driven upper-limb exoskeleton enabling voluntary elbow and wrist movements using EEG-based steady-state visual evoked potentials.

Project overview

Individuals affected by upper-limb motor impairments such as hemiplegia, upper limb monoplegia, and quadriplegia experience severe limitations in independence and daily functionality. Hemiplegia restricts movement on one side of the body, monoplegia affects a single limb, while quadriplegia results in paralysis of all four limbs, commonly due to cervical spinal cord injuries.

Assistive robotic exoskeletons have demonstrated strong potential in restoring functional motion and supporting rehabilitation. This project focuses on the design and development of a brain-controlled upper-limb exoskeleton arm that enables voluntary arm movement through non-invasive brain–computer interface (BCI) technology.

Objectives

EEG signal acquisition

Acquire brain signals using electroencephalography (EEG) and evaluate multiple paradigms including SSVEP, Motor Imagery, ERD/ERS, and P300 potentials.

Signal processing & classification

Filter, transform, and classify EEG signals to reliably extract user intent for real-time control.

Exoskeleton design

Design and develop a two-joint upper-limb exoskeleton capable of controlled elbow and wrist movements.

BCI integration

Integrate the BCI system with the exoskeleton hardware to enable hands-free, brain-driven actuation.

Methodology

The system primarily employs the steady-state visual evoked potential (SSVEP) method to acquire EEG signals. Four distinct visual stimulus frequencies are presented using LED matrices, each corresponding to a specific movement command.

The SSVEP setup is implemented using four LED arrays driven by an STM32 microcontroller. EEG signals are processed through a multi-stage pipeline including bandpass filtering, notch filtering, frequency-domain analysis, peak detection, and harmonic validation.

Functional capabilities

The developed exoskeleton arm is capable of executing four controlled movements, one at a time, to predefined angular positions:

Elbow flexion Elbow extension Wrist flexion Wrist extension

The system is designed with a focus on practical daily tasks, particularly enabling actions such as supporting and resting objects on the user’s palm.

Key words

Brain computer interface (BCI) Electroencephalography (EEG) Steady state visual evoked potential (SSVEP) Motor imagery (MI) Assistive robotics Rehabilitation engineering
